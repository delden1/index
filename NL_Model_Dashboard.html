Hybrid Merit Order Stack Model — Netherlands 2023

Interactive dashboard for a tranche-based electricity price model calibrated against EPEX day-ahead prices using Bayesian optimisation. The model captures cross-border flows, CHP must-run dynamics, and fuel cost pass-through to evaluate storage arbitrage economics.

What does this model do?

This model simulates Dutch wholesale electricity prices by constructing a Merit Order Stack from the actual NL power plant fleet and dispatching generators against hourly residual demand (total load minus wind, solar, and net imports). Instead of treating each plant as a single price-quantity pair, we apply 9-tranche bid spreading (Ward et al. 2019) — splitting each generator's capacity into tranches that bid at different fractions of their marginal cost. This smooths the supply curve and produces more realistic price distributions, including negative prices from CHP must-run generation.

The tranche parameters are calibrated against real EPEX day-ahead prices using three methods: Monte Carlo random search, Bayesian optimisation (Optuna TPE), and SciPy Nelder-Mead. The calibrated model is then used to evaluate how accurately storage arbitrage revenues can be estimated — the key output for investment decisions.

How is energy trading incorporated?

In the real EPEX day-ahead market, generators submit price-quantity bid pairs for each hour of the next day. The market operator (EPEX SPOT) stacks all bids from cheapest to most expensive, forming a supply curve. Where this supply curve intersects demand, the market-clearing price is set — and every accepted generator receives this price, not their own bid.

Our model replicates this mechanism. Each plant's Short-Run Marginal Cost (SRMC = fuel price ÷ efficiency + CO₂ cost) determines its base bid. But in reality, generators don't bid exactly at SRMC — they engage in strategic bidding: some tranches bid below cost to guarantee dispatch, others bid above cost to capture scarcity rents. The 9-tranche spreading models precisely this behaviour — splitting each plant's capacity into tranches bidding at 45% below to 109% above SRMC. This is why the tranche model produces a smooth, realistic supply curve instead of a sharp staircase.

Negative prices occur when CHP plants (combined heat & power) must run to supply district heating in winter, regardless of electricity prices. They bid at negative prices (down to −150 EUR/MWh) to stay dispatched. When enough CHP + wind + solar exceeds demand, the market price turns negative — a real phenomenon visible in 3.6% of 2023 hours. Cross-border flows from neighbouring countries (Germany, Belgium, Denmark, Norway, UK) are incorporated as a demand adjustment: exports increase the residual demand NL plants must serve, imports decrease it.

Netherlands Power Plant Fleet (2023)

The fleet was verified against actual Energy-Charts generation data.

Data Pipeline

All data was collected from public sources and aligned to hourly resolution for the full year 2023 (8,760 hours). 15-minute generation and flow data from Energy-Charts is resampled to hourly means. Daily fuel prices are forward-filled to hourly. API2 coal prices are converted from USD/tonne to EUR/MWh using daily ECB exchange rates and the IEA standard energy content (6.978 MWh/tonne).

Cross-border Physical Flows

The Netherlands is interconnected with 5 neighbours: Belgium, Germany, Denmark, Norway, and the United Kingdom. In 2023, NL was a net exporter (mean −625 MW), largely driven by strong gas-fired generation and high interconnector capacity. These flows are treated as a demand adjustment in the model: net exports increase residual demand that domestic plants must serve, while net imports reduce it. This approach avoids needing to model neighbouring country fleets while still capturing the price impact of cross-border trade.

Calibration Methodology

The 22-parameter tranche model is calibrated by minimising a weighted objective function combining RMSE, volatility ratio, tail accuracy (P10/P90), and correlation. Three optimisation methods are compared: Monte Carlo (random sampling), Bayesian optimisation via Optuna's Tree-structured Parzen Estimator (TPE), and SciPy Nelder-Mead (gradient-free local search). The key finding is that Bayesian TPE achieves the best result with fewer evaluations — this is the novel methodological contribution of the thesis.

Storage Arbitrage Analysis

The ultimate test of a price model: can it predict storage revenue accurately? We simulate a greedy buy-low/sell-high arbitrage strategy across four battery technologies using both observed EPEX prices and the calibrated model prices. The revenue capture ratio (model profit ÷ observed profit) shows how well the model preserves the economic signal that drives investment decisions.

Parameter Sensitivity Analysis

How sensitive is the model to changes in key input parameters? This analysis runs 28 scenarios across 6 categories — varying plant efficiency, fuel prices, CO₂ price, CHP must-run capacity, cross-border flows, and tranche spreading parameters. Each scenario uses the calibrated baseline and perturbs one variable at a time. The results reveal which "levers" have the biggest impact on price accuracy, and which parameters the model is robust to.

Key finding: The model is most sensitive to CHP must-run capacity and gas prices — these are the dominant price drivers in the Dutch market. Coal price changes have almost no effect because coal plants are rarely marginal in the Netherlands.
